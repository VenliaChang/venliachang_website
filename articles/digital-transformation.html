數位轉型失敗，從來不是技術問題 Why Digital Transformation Stalls — It's Never a Technology Problem

Venlia Chang
2025
4 分鐘閱讀

做了這麼多年，我發現一件事：能同時站在技術和業務兩端、讓雙方都聽得進去的人，真的很少。

一個我見過太多次的場景

公司斥資引進工具，請了數據團隊，老闆宣示數位轉型的決心。三個月後，系統上線了，報表出來了——但決策還是跟以前一樣。沒有人知道問題出在哪裡。

問題不在技術。

工程師和數據人看到的世界

他們看到的是：資料品質很差、需求一直改、老闆根本不懂他們在做什麼。

他們的目標是：把系統做正確、把模型準確度拉高、把架構建得穩。

這些都對。但這不是老闆要的。

老闆看到的世界

他們看到的是：花了這麼多錢，為什麼還是看不到結果？為什麼每次問問題都要等兩週？為什麼報表這麼難懂？

他們的目標是：讓決策更快、更準、更有依據。

這也對。但沒有人把這個目標翻譯成技術語言。

兩邊都沒有錯，但沒有人在說同一件事

工程師做了他被交代的事。老闆表達了他的期望。但兩件事從來沒有對上——而且彼此都不知道。

於是專案延遲、預算追加、成果模糊。每個人都有說法，沒有人推進，也沒有人承擔。

這不是技術問題。這是人的問題。

真正缺的是什麼

一個能同時做到這些的人：

  • 聽懂老闆說的「我想要更好的能見度」，翻譯成可以執行的技術需求。
  • 把工程師做出來的東西，解釋成高層能用來決策的語言。
  • 在兩邊各自有立場的時候，找出共同利益，讓專案繼續走。

這不只是溝通技巧。這需要你真的懂兩個世界——技術上能自己動手，業務上能站在經營層的角度思考。

為什麼這種人這麼少？

因為這兩套能力，幾乎從來不在同一條路上建立。

走技術路線的人，很少有機會真正進入業務決策的核心。走業務路線的人，很少有能力自己把系統做出來。即使兩種都懂，還要有意願在組織裡穿針引線、推動對齊——這又是另一門學問。

所以大多數公司選擇請顧問公司。但顧問走了之後，問題又回來了。

數位轉型真正需要的

不是更好的工具。不是更多的數據。不是更大的預算。

是一個真的懂你公司問題、能設計解法、能把它做出來、又能讓組織真正用起來的人。

這種人很少。但他們才是讓轉型真正發生的關鍵。

After years in this field, I've come to one clear observation: the person who can stand in both worlds — technical and business — and make both sides feel understood, is genuinely rare.

A scene I've watched play out too many times

A company invests in tools. Builds a data team. Leadership announces the digital transformation initiative with conviction. Three months later, dashboards are live, models are running — and decisions are made exactly the same way they were before. Nobody can explain why.

The technology worked. The problem wasn't the technology.

What engineers and data people see

They see: poor data quality, shifting requirements, stakeholders who don't understand what they're building.

Their goal: technically correct systems, accurate models, robust architecture.

All of this is legitimate. None of it is what the business actually asked for.

What executives see

They see: money spent, time passed, nothing visibly different. Why does every question take two weeks to answer? Why are the reports impossible to read?

Their goal: faster decisions, better grounded, more defensible.

Also legitimate. But nobody translated that goal into something an engineer could act on.

Both sides are right — and nobody is talking about the same thing

The engineer delivered what was specified. The executive expressed what they needed. The two never connected — and neither side realized it.

So projects slip. Budgets expand. Outcomes stay vague. Everyone has an explanation. Nobody drives it forward.

This isn't a technology failure. It's a people failure.

What's actually missing

Someone who can do all of this:

  • Hear what an executive means by "I want better visibility" and translate it into a buildable technical specification.
  • Take what an engineer built and explain it in terms a leadership team can act on.
  • Navigate the competing agendas — and find the shared interest that keeps the project moving.

This isn't a communication skill. It requires genuinely inhabiting both worlds — technically capable enough to build it yourself, commercially fluent enough to think like the person making the decision.

Why this person is so rare

Because these two capabilities are almost never built on the same path.

Technical training rarely puts you inside real business decision-making. Business experience rarely gives you the depth to build the system yourself. And even if both are present, the organizational navigation — keeping initiatives alive across competing interests — is a third skill set that neither background teaches.

So most companies default to consulting firms. But the consultants leave. And the problem comes back.

What digital transformation actually requires

Not better tools. Not more data. Not a larger budget.

A person who genuinely understands the business problem, can design the solution, can build it themselves, and can embed it into the organization so it actually gets used.

That person is rare. But they're the reason transformations succeed or fail.
